About the Model

This model was created to take in information from bills in the United States House of Representatives and Senate that made it to a vote and make predictions on whether currently introduced bills will pass or fail an upcoming vote based on the same criteria. When using the model, the assumption must be made that the bill will remain as is, meaning that the model is basing its predictions off the bills' current information, and it will not account for changes variables, such as an increased or decreased number of cosponsors.

Model Plot

This model uses scikit-learn and TensorFlow/Keras packages for Python. After preprocessing and splitting data into train and test data, the model was trained and tested. This model shows an accuracy score of 96.72% for the House and 96.35% for the Senate.

The two graphs above show the total number of bills that passed and failed for each iteration of Congress from the 113th to the 117th for each chamber. The amount of bills passed for the House of Representatives has remained fairly consistent throughout, whereas the amount of failed bills has increased. This would show that more bills were introduced in the 117th Congress, but passing rate remains similar to previous iterations of Congress. Bills passed in the House of Represenatives are sent to the Senate to be reviewed, amended, and voted on. As shown above, the number of bills shown for the 116th and 117th iteration of the Senate has decreased dramatically from the previous three iterations. Furthermore, there is no representation from the Democratic party in cosponsorships for these bills. This is an interesting observation, considering that the Democratic Party controlled the House of Represenatives during the 116th and 117th iteration of Congress.

The 116th and 117th Congress's House of Representatives showed a higher amount of cosponsors attached to bills that have passed compared to the previous three iterations. There is also a higher overall average amount of Democrat cosponsors in the more recent iterations of the House.

Based on our findings, bills introduced in January have the highest passing rate in the House, and bills introduced in March have the highest passing rate in the Senate. Passing rates for both chambers fall significantly after the first half of the year.

Unlike the House of Respresentatives, the Senate chamber for the 117th Congress shows significantly less average cosponsors than the previous few iterations. The amount of average cosponsors among bills that have failed remains fairly consistent across both chambers for all five iterations of Congress viewed here.
